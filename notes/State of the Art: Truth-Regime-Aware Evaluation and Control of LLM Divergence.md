
Executive Summary
Large language models generate text along a spectrum from strict factual reproduction to unconstrained invention, yet current evaluation and mitigation systems overwhelmingly treat any departure from a ground-truth reference as error. This literature review surveys three interconnected research axes that, taken together, lay the groundwork for a paradigm shift: from binary hallucination detection toward regime-aware evaluation and control of LLM divergence. Axis A examines how the implicit truth contract of a prompt — what must be true, what may be speculated, and what may be invented — can be inferred, represented, and disambiguated. Axis B investigates productive divergence: the conditions under which speculative or non-grounded generation improves problem-solving, and the mechanisms that convert exploratory outputs into reliable results. Axis C reviews hallucination detection and mitigation pipelines, assessing the extent to which they accommodate prompt-dependent truth requirements and distinguish harmful errors from acceptable departures.
The review is structured around the conceptual vocabulary introduced in the position paper "Hallucination Is Relative" (Carlesso, submitted to UAI 2026): Truth Regime \(\text{TR}(p) = (O, \sigma, \kappa)\), comprising an oracle \(O\), a stylistic latitude parameter \(\sigma\), and a factual content invention parameter \(\kappa\); Creative Intent (CI); Productive Divergence (PD); and Hallucination (H) as a violation of the operative regime. Across all three axes, a consistent finding emerges: existing work addresses fragments of the truth-regime problem — intent classification, creativity benchmarking, claim-level verification — but no unified framework conditions detection and evaluation on the regime expected by the user. This gap defines the central opportunity for the thesis research program.


1. Axis A — Inferring the Implicit Truth Regime of a Prompt
1.1 Defining and Representing the Truth Regime (RQ-A1)
No prior work explicitly formalizes a "truth regime" in the sense of \(\text{TR}(p) = (O, \sigma, \kappa)\), but several lines of research approximate components of this construct. The most direct precursor is the distinction between intrinsic and extrinsic hallucination established in the NLG hallucination literature. Ji et al. (2023) define intrinsic hallucination as output that contradicts the source and extrinsic hallucination as output that introduces unverifiable new information (Ji et al., 2023). This binary split implicitly partitions the space of permissible generation but does not parameterize it by task or user intent. Alansari & Luqman (2025) extend this taxonomy by distinguishing factuality hallucination (divergence from real-world facts) from faithfulness hallucination (drift from input/context), further subdivided into instruction inconsistency, context inconsistency, and logical inconsistency (Alansari & Luqman, 2025). The faithfulness–factuality distinction maps loosely onto the \(\kappa\) dimension (permission to introduce new factual content) versus fidelity to a source oracle \(O\).
Li et al. (2026) sharpen this tension by formalizing Harmful Factuality Hallucination (HFH): cases where an LLM "corrects" a factual error in the source, producing an output that is factually true but unfaithful to the input (Li et al., 2026). HFH demonstrates that the oracle \(O\) can itself be ambiguous — the user may expect fidelity to a flawed source (low \(\kappa\), fixed \(O\)) or correction toward world knowledge (high \(\kappa\), external \(O\)). Li et al. show that HFH worsens with model scale and that a simple instructional prompt reduces HFH rates by approximately 50%, suggesting that regime specification in the prompt has measurable effects on output behavior.
A complementary representational approach comes from internal model geometry. Marks & Tegmark (2024) demonstrate that LLMs linearly represent the truth or falsehood of factual statements in their internal activations, with simple difference-in-mean probes generalizing across datasets and being causally implicated in model outputs (Marks & Tegmark, 2024). This finding suggests that truth-regime states may be detectable not only from prompt features but also from the model's own latent representations — a direction highly relevant to operationalizing \(O\) as a model-internal construct.
1.2 Observable Features for Truth-Regime Inference (RQ-A2)
Several prompt-level and interaction-level features have been shown to correlate with the factual constraints implicitly expected by users. These features can be organized into three layers: lexical and structural cues in the prompt itself, interaction-level signals from multi-turn dialogue, and model-internal representations.
User intent taxonomies. Bodonhelyi et al. (2024) develop a fine-grained intent taxonomy for LLM interactions (informational, problem-solving, creative, educational, among others) and show that GPT-4 outperforms GPT-3.5 on common intents but underperforms on less frequent intents (Bodonhelyi et al., 2024). Shah et al. (2023) propose an LLM-based pipeline for generating, validating, and applying user intent taxonomies with human-in-the-loop verification, demonstrating effectiveness on Bing search and chat logs (Shah et al., 2023). Both works partition intent space into categories that carry different implicit truth expectations — an informational query demands high factual fidelity (low \(\kappa\)), whereas a creative query permits invention (high \(\kappa\)) — but neither maps intents onto explicit factual constraints. The gap is significant: an intent label like "creative" does not specify whether the user expects factually grounded creativity (\(\kappa\) low, \(\sigma\) high — e.g., a stylistically inventive but factually accurate science article) or unconstrained fiction (\(\kappa\) high, \(\sigma\) high).
Prompt clarity and specificity. Dang et al. (2025) introduce the metrics Prompt Sensitivity (PS) and Model Variability (MV) to disentangle prompt-induced from model-intrinsic hallucination. PS measures variation in hallucination rates across prompt styles for a fixed model, while MV measures variation across models for a fixed prompt (Dang et al., 2025). Crucially, vague prompts yield the highest hallucination rates (38.3% on average), whereas specific prompts satisfying a Prompt Clarity Score (PCS) of \(\geq 5/6\) on a clarity checklist significantly reduce hallucinations. Chain-of-thought prompting further reduces Conditional Prompt Sensitivity from 0.15 to 0.06. These findings confirm that prompt features are predictive of hallucination risk, but they treat hallucination as uniformly undesirable rather than conditioning acceptability on regime.
Instruction compliance. Tripathi et al. (2025) evaluate 13 LLMs on instruction compliance in enterprise RAG scenarios across 600 queries and five enterprise personas. They document an "instruction gap" where models that excel at general benchmarks struggle with specific enterprise instruction requirements, with violation counts ranging from 660 (GPT-5) to 1,330 (Gemini 2.0-Flash) (Tripathi et al., 2025). Importantly, they find that instruction compliance and answer accuracy are partially decoupled: models following all instructions may not provide accurate answers, and highly accurate models may violate formatting and scope constraints. This directly mirrors the \(\sigma\)/\(\kappa\) decomposition — a model may satisfy factual requirements while violating stylistic ones, or vice versa. The enterprise persona dimension is also relevant: different personas (IT Support Agent, Teacher's Support Agent, Product Support Agent) carry different implicit truth regimes, with IT support demanding precision and product support permitting more interpretive latitude. Yet the evaluation treats all violations equivalently rather than weighting them by regime.
Model-internal features. Beyond prompt-observable features, internal model states carry regime-relevant information. Marks & Tegmark's (2024) finding that truth is linearly represented in LLM activations (Marks & Tegmark, 2024) suggests that the model's own confidence about factual content — a proxy for whether the generation is grounded or speculative — can be extracted from hidden states. Kossen et al.'s (2024) semantic entropy similarly captures whether the model is generating consistent (low uncertainty, likely factual) or variable (high uncertainty, possibly speculative) content across samples (Kossen et al., 2024). These internal signals could complement prompt-level features in a multi-modal regime inference system: the prompt provides the expected regime, while internal states indicate the actual generation regime, enabling detection of mismatches.
1.3 Distinguishing Stylistic Creativity from Factual License (RQ-A3)
The position paper's key innovation is separating stylistic latitude \(\sigma\) from factual content invention \(\kappa\). Existing work rarely makes this distinction explicit, although several lines of research provide indirect evidence for its importance.
Zhou et al. (2023) propose context-faithful prompting, identifying opinion-based prompts (reframing context as a narrator's statement) and counterfactual demonstrations (instances containing false facts) as the most effective methods for improving LLM faithfulness to context (Zhou et al., 2023). Opinion-based prompts essentially modify the perceived \(O\) from world knowledge to a narrator's perspective without changing \(\sigma\), demonstrated on machine reading comprehension and relation extraction tasks without requiring additional training. This shows that prompt framing can shift the truth regime along the factual dimension independently of style. Counterfactual demonstrations, by contrast, train the model to prioritize context over parametric knowledge — effectively lowering \(\kappa\) by constraining the factual invention space to the provided source.
The HFH framework of Li et al. (2026) provides perhaps the clearest empirical demonstration of the \(\sigma\)/\(\kappa\) tension: in rephrasing tasks, the model may restyle text (exercise \(\sigma\)) while simultaneously "correcting" facts (exercising \(\kappa\) without permission), producing outputs that are stylistically appropriate but factually unfaithful (Li et al., 2026). Li et al. further show that HFH worsens with model scale — a troubling finding suggesting that more capable models are more likely to confuse stylistic and factual permissions. The FAITHQA benchmark similarly reveals that existing evaluations conflate stylistic and factual departures, since the Intent Constraint metric evaluates constraint satisfaction holistically rather than separating the two dimensions (Hao et al., 2025).
The comprehensive hallucination survey by Alansari & Luqman (2025) introduces the faithfulness hallucination subcategory of "instruction inconsistency" — failures to follow user instructions regarding format, structure, or scope — which corresponds closely to \(\sigma\) violations, distinct from their "context inconsistency" subcategory (closer to \(\kappa\) violations) (Alansari & Luqman, 2025). However, this distinction remains taxonomic rather than operational: no evaluation metric separately scores \(\sigma\)-violations and \(\kappa\)-violations, which is precisely what the TR framework would enable.
1.4 Ambiguity Detection and Active Clarification (RQ-A4)
When the truth regime is underdetermined, systems must either default to a conservative regime or actively seek clarification. Several approaches address this.
Ambiguity detection and clarification question generation. Lee et al. (2023) introduce the CAMBIGNQ dataset of 5,653 ambiguous user questions with a three-stage pipeline: (1) ambiguity detection via binary classification, (2) clarification question generation (CQ), and (3) clarification-based QA. Their best ambiguity detection model achieves 63.9% accuracy, and the CQ-based answering pipeline reaches 55.8 F1 when using ground-truth CQs (Lee et al., 2023). The gap between ground-truth and generated CQ performance (55.8 vs. 40.5 F1) highlights the difficulty of automated ambiguity resolution.
Proactive clarification. Wang et al. (2023) propose Proactive Chain-of-Thought (ProCoT) prompting, which triggers LLMs' reasoning about ambiguity before deciding whether to ask a clarification question. On the Abg-CoQA benchmark, one-shot ProCoT with ChatGPT achieves 27.9 F1 and 45.9% helpfulness, outperforming standard prompting (Wang et al., 2023). However, domain-specific performance remains limited (35.8% vs. 80.1% SOTA on PACIFIC Finance), indicating that clarification capability degrades in specialized truth regimes.
Domain-specific clarification. Darji & Lutellier (2025) build an LLM-based coding assistant with a query classifier trained to detect ambiguous programming queries and a fine-tuned LLM that generates clarification questions. The fine-tuned model outperforms zero-shot baselines in user studies, demonstrating that domain-specific clarification is feasible (Darji & Lutellier, 2025).
Internal uncertainty as a proxy. The APA (Alignment with Perceived Ambiguity) approach uses model self-checks on uncertainty, internally disambiguating and measuring information gain to decide whether to trigger clarification. The CLAMBER study reveals that models struggle to identify ambiguous queries and that Chain-of-Thought prompting does not reliably help, suggesting that ambiguity detection remains an unsolved problem for current architectures.
1.5 Limitations and Open Challenges (RQ-A5)
The literature on truth-regime inference faces several structural limitations:

No unified representation. Intent taxonomies, prompt clarity scores, and instruction compliance metrics each capture a facet of the truth regime but remain disconnected. There is no formalism analogous to \(\text{TR}(p) = (O, \sigma, \kappa)\) that integrates these dimensions.
Gold-label instability. As Bodonhelyi et al. (2024) observe, even when intent is correctly recognized, users are more satisfied with original prompts than intent-reformulated ones (Bodonhelyi et al., 2024), suggesting that user intent is often implicit, multi-layered, or inconsistent — a fundamental challenge for supervised approaches.
Binary treatment of hallucination. All surveyed prompt-sensitivity studies (Dang et al., 2025; Tripathi et al., 2025) measure hallucination as a binary or scalar defect rate rather than conditioning acceptability on the inferred regime. This makes it impossible to distinguish a creative departure that satisfies a permissive regime from a factual error that violates a strict one.
Limited clarification capabilities. Ambiguity detection accuracy remains modest (63.9% for CAMBIGNQ), and domain transfer is poor. No existing system links clarification to regime specification — asking "do you want factual accuracy or creative elaboration?" rather than "which entity do you mean?"
Absence of \(\sigma\)/\(\kappa\) decomposition. No prior work explicitly separates stylistic freedom from factual permission, making it difficult to build regime-aware evaluation.
1.6 Positioning Relative to the Position Paper
The position paper's formalization of \(\text{TR}(p) = (O, \sigma, \kappa)\) addresses the central gap identified in this axis: the lack of a unified representation that integrates oracle specification, stylistic latitude, and factual invention permission. The empirical finding that writing format is the largest driver of hallucination rate (+21.4%) while CI raises hallucination by +12.8% provides the first quantitative evidence for the \(\sigma\)/\(\kappa\) decomposition. The research agenda's call for "automatic TR inference from prompts" maps directly onto the gap between existing intent taxonomies (which classify broadly) and regime-aware inference (which must predict per-claim constraints). The publishable angle — moving from exact TR prediction to ambiguity detection and active clarification — is well-supported by the demonstrated limitations of current clarification systems and the instability of gold labels.


2. Axis B — Productive Divergence in Problem Solving
2.1 Tasks Where Speculative Generation Helps (RQ-B1)
A growing body of work documents tasks where non-grounded or speculative generation measurably improves outcomes, spanning code generation, mathematical reasoning, creative ideation, and scientific hypothesis formation.
Code generation and repair. Tang et al. (2024) formalize code repair with LLMs as an exploration-exploitation tradeoff, modeled as an arm-acquiring bandit problem solved with Thompson Sampling. The key insight is that refining only the best-performing program (exploitation) is suboptimal; exploring lesser-considered programs (exploration) discovers better solutions (Tang et al., 2024). This directly instantiates productive divergence: generating speculative code variants — some of which are locally "wrong" — expands the search space and yields superior final programs after verification.
Mathematical reasoning. Sun et al. (2025) introduce the Enumerate-Conjecture-Prove (ECP) framework, a modular neuro-symbolic method that integrates LLM-based enumeration and pattern-driven conjecturing with formal theorem proving in Lean. On PutnamBench, ECP formally solves 6 out of 337 answer-construction problems end-to-end (up from 4 without ECP), demonstrating that LLM "hallucinations" — conjectures not yet verified — serve as productive hypotheses when coupled with formal verification (Sun et al., 2025). Zakhalili et al. (2025) complement this by analyzing intermediate reasoning in code-assisted LLMs, finding that even "brute-force" and "no-logic" programs — which constitute unsound reasoning — achieve comparable execution accuracy to conceptually grounded programs, particularly on challenging datasets like MATH500 (Zakhalili et al., 2025). This suggests that unsound intermediate reasoning, while not trustworthy, can function as a productive heuristic under verification.
Scientific ideation. Ruan et al. (2026) introduce LiveIdeaBench, a benchmark evaluating divergent thinking for scientific idea generation across 1,180 keywords spanning 22 scientific domains with over 40 LLMs, assessed on originality, feasibility, fluency, flexibility, and clarity (Ruan et al., 2026). The framework draws on Guilford's creativity theory and demonstrates that LLMs can generate genuinely novel scientific hypotheses, though with variable feasibility — a textbook case of productive divergence requiring subsequent convergence.
Creative writing and divergent thinking. Olson et al. (2026) use the Divergent Association Task (DAT) to compare 100,000 humans with leading LLMs, finding that GPT-4 surpasses the human average but not the top decile, with temperature significantly modulating creativity (DAT score rising from default to 85.6 at temperature 1.5, exceeding 72% of humans) (Olson et al., 2026). Higher temperature increases divergent semantic distance but reduces coherence — a direct empirical manifestation of the creativity–hallucination tradeoff.
2.2 Separating Productive Divergence from Harmful Hallucination (RQ-B2)
The central conceptual challenge for Axis B is distinguishing inventions that improve outcomes (PD) from inventions that degrade them (H). Several frameworks address this.
The divergent–convergent thinking framework. Jiang et al. (2024) propose viewing hallucination through the lens of divergent and convergent thinking, arguing that hallucinations may serve as creativity catalysts during the divergent phase and should only be evaluated as errors during the convergent phase (Jiang et al., 2024). This two-phase model aligns with the position paper's concept of productive divergence: an intermediate generation is PD if it occurs in a regime where \(\kappa\) permits invention and a convergence mechanism subsequently enforces correctness.
Empirical evidence from hallucination-reduction methods. Banerjee et al. (2025) provide the most direct evidence that the hallucination–creativity relationship is method-dependent. Evaluating Chain of Verification (CoVe), Decoding by Contrasting Layers (DoLa), and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) across LLaMA, Qwen, and Mistral families (1B–70B parameters) on the NeoCoder and CS4 creativity benchmarks, they find that CoVe enhances divergent thinking, DoLa suppresses it, and RAG shows minimal impact (Banerjee et al., 2025). This demonstrates that hallucination mitigation is not uniformly anti-creative — the mechanism matters, and some approaches preserve or even enhance productive divergence while reducing harmful hallucination.
Layer-wise creativity–hallucination tradeoff. He et al. (2025) introduce the HCL framework, quantifying hallucination and creativity across different layers of LLMs during decoding. They identify a consistent tradeoff across layer depth, model type, and model size, and find a specific layer at each model size that optimally balances creativity and hallucination. Notably, the optimal layer tends to appear in early layers for larger models, with higher model confidence at these layers (He et al., 2025). This layer-wise analysis suggests that PD and H may be mechanistically distinguishable within the model architecture.
Logical soundness taxonomy. Zakhalili et al. (2025) propose a six-class taxonomy for code-assisted reasoning: three sound classes (conceptual programs, primitive programs, from-scratch implementations) and three unsound classes (brute-force, disorganized, no-logic). The finding that unsound programs achieve comparable accuracy to sound ones on challenging benchmarks (Zakhalili et al., 2025) poses a definitional challenge: if the output is correct, is unsound intermediate reasoning a hallucination or productive divergence? The answer depends on whether one evaluates the process or the product — and by extension, on the regime.
2.3 Joint Evaluation of Novelty, Usefulness, and Correctness (RQ-B3)
Evaluating productive divergence requires metrics that simultaneously capture novelty, usefulness, and correctness — a multi-objective problem that existing benchmarks address incompletely.
CreativityPrism. Hou et al. (2025) propose CreativityPrism, a framework consolidating eight tasks from three domains (divergent thinking, creative writing, logical reasoning) into a taxonomy emphasizing quality, novelty, and diversity. Evaluating 17 LLMs, they find that high performance in one creative dimension rarely generalizes to others, and novelty metrics often show weak or negative correlations with quality metrics (Hou et al., 2025). This fragmentation confirms that a single "creativity score" is insufficient — evaluation must be multi-dimensional, paralleling the multi-dimensional nature of \(\text{TR}(p)\).
Automated creativity evaluation. The TTCW-based automated evaluation framework uses 14 binary tests across four dimensions (fluency, flexibility, originality, elaboration), with reference-based Likert-style scoring that mitigates LLM sycophancy by anchoring evaluations to high-quality reference texts. The approach achieves 0.75 pairwise accuracy and demonstrates that reference-based evaluation outperforms reference-free methods in calibration and discrimination (Automated Creativity Evaluation, 2025). This methodology could be adapted for regime-aware evaluation by conditioning the reference on the expected truth regime.
LiveIdeaBench. The five-dimensional assessment (originality, feasibility, fluency, flexibility, clarity) used by LiveIdeaBench across 22 scientific domains provides a domain-specific instantiation of joint evaluation, demonstrating that novelty without feasibility is insufficient for productive scientific ideation (Ruan et al., 2026).
FActScore and SAFE. While primarily designed for factuality, the atomic fact decomposition approach of FActScore (Min et al., 2023) and the GPT-4-based claim-wise verification of SAFE (Wei et al., 2024) provide the granularity needed for joint evaluation: each atomic claim can be independently assessed for both factual accuracy and novelty relative to the source.
2.4 Evidence for Beneficial Exploration Before Verification (RQ-B4)
The explore-then-verify paradigm is best evidenced in formal domains with automatic verifiers, but emerging evidence extends to creative and scientific domains as well.
Code repair. The arm-acquiring bandit formalization of Tang et al. (2024) provides rigorous evidence: Thompson Sampling over diverse code candidates outperforms greedy refinement of the best candidate, with the margin increasing for harder problems (Tang et al., 2024). The "exploration" phase generates candidates that would be labeled as hallucinations by a binary detector, yet they are essential for discovering the solution. In the TR framework, this corresponds to a high-\(\kappa\) intermediate phase (where the model is permitted to generate diverse, possibly incorrect, solutions) followed by a low-\(\kappa\) convergence phase (where only test-passing solutions are retained).
Mathematical conjecturing. The ECP framework explicitly separates the exploratory phase (LLM enumeration and conjecturing) from the verification phase (formal proof in Lean), achieving state-of-the-art accuracy of 33.1% on ConstructiveBench with a dataset of 3,640 problems (Sun et al., 2025). The key architectural insight is that the LLM's conjectures need not be correct — they need only be checkable — transforming hallucination into a feature of the discovery pipeline. This neuro-symbolic decomposition provides a clean instantiation of the TR framework: the conjecturing module operates under a permissive TR with high \(\kappa\), while the proving module enforces a strict TR with \(\kappa = 0\) (only provable claims pass).
Temperature as exploration control. Olson et al. (2026) demonstrate that increasing temperature from 0.5 to 1.5 raises GPT-4's DAT score significantly, with the effect most pronounced in less structured creative formats (flash fiction DSI increases significantly at \(p < .001\)) but absent in highly constrained formats (haikus show no effect) (Olson et al., 2026). This format-dependent effect parallels the position paper's finding that writing format is the largest driver of hallucination rate: both demonstrate that exploration benefits are mediated by the task's structural constraints. In TR terms, temperature functions as a crude proxy for \(\kappa\) — higher temperature expands the space of candidate tokens, permitting more factual invention — but it lacks the selectivity of a regime-aware system that could increase \(\kappa\) for some claims while constraining others.
Decoding-level control. Potraghloo et al. (2025) introduce Top-H decoding, formulating an entropy-constrained minimum divergence problem to balance creativity and coherence at the token level. Top-H outperforms min-\(p\) sampling by up to 25.63% on creative writing benchmarks while maintaining robustness on factual QA (Potraghloo et al., 2025). The entropy bound serves as a formal analog of \(\sigma + \kappa\): higher entropy bounds permit more divergent generation, while lower bounds enforce coherence. However, Top-H applies a uniform entropy constraint across all tokens rather than conditioning on per-claim regime expectations.
Scientific ideation. LiveIdeaBench's evaluation of 40+ models across 22 scientific domains shows that LLMs can generate original scientific ideas (divergent exploration) that are also assessed for feasibility (a form of convergent verification) (Ruan et al., 2026). The five-dimensional assessment (originality, feasibility, fluency, flexibility, clarity) constitutes an implicit explore-verify framework where originality rewards exploration and feasibility rewards convergence.
2.5 Convergence Mechanisms: From Speculation to Reliable Outputs (RQ-B5)
Productive divergence requires convergence mechanisms that filter, verify, or refine speculative outputs into reliable final answers.
Formal verification. The ECP framework and the code repair bandit framework both use executable verification (Lean proof checker, test suite execution) as hard convergence mechanisms. These guarantee that final outputs meet formal correctness criteria regardless of the quality of intermediate speculation (Sun et al., 2025; Tang et al., 2024).
Self-verification chains. Banerjee et al. (2025) show that Chain of Verification (CoVe) simultaneously reduces hallucination and enhances creativity, suggesting that self-verification acts as a convergence mechanism that preserves productive divergence while filtering harmful hallucination (Banerjee et al., 2025). In contrast, DoLa's layer-contrasting approach suppresses both hallucination and creativity, functioning as an overly aggressive convergence mechanism.
Layer selection. He et al. (2025) suggest that selecting the optimal decoding layer — which balances creativity and hallucination — could serve as an architectural convergence mechanism. The finding that this optimal layer appears earlier in larger models suggests that scale enables earlier commitment to meaning while preserving stylistic flexibility (He et al., 2025).
Entropy-based gating. Semantic entropy (Kossen et al., 2024) can serve as a convergence gate: high semantic entropy indicates the model is uncertain about the meaning of its output, warranting either further exploration or refusal. Low semantic entropy indicates convergence to a consistent meaning, regardless of surface-level variation (Kossen et al., 2024). This directly implements a \(\sigma\)-aware check: variation in wording with consistent meaning (high \(\sigma\), low semantic entropy) is acceptable, whereas variation in meaning (high \(\kappa\) under a restrictive regime) is flagged.
2.6 Positioning Relative to the Position Paper
The position paper's concept of Productive Divergence (PD) fills a precise gap in the literature: existing work either treats all hallucination as error or celebrates it as creativity, without providing a principled boundary conditioned on regime. The empirical evidence reviewed here confirms that exploration improves outcomes in code, mathematics, and ideation, but the boundary between PD and H depends on (a) the task's tolerance for factual invention (\(\kappa\)), (b) the availability of a convergence mechanism, and (c) the evaluation phase (intermediate vs. final output). The position paper's finding that format-specific factual constraints moderate the creativity effect (+21.4% for format, moderated by format-specific \(\kappa\)) provides the first empirical test of this regime-dependent boundary. The research agenda's call for "controlling H without collapsing PD" maps onto the mechanism-selection problem evidenced by Banerjee et al.'s finding that CoVe preserves creativity while DoLa destroys it.


3. Axis C — Regime-Aware Hallucination Detection and Mitigation
3.1 How Current Methods Define and Localize Errors (RQ-C1)
The hallucination detection landscape has evolved from document-level binary classification toward claim-level atomic verification, but the definition of "error" remains uniformly binary.
Taxonomic foundations. Ji et al. (2023) provide the foundational taxonomy distinguishing intrinsic hallucination (contradicts source) from extrinsic hallucination (introduces unsupported content), covering summarization, dialogue, QA, data-to-text, and machine translation (Ji et al., 2023). Alansari & Luqman (2025) extend this to factuality hallucination vs. faithfulness hallucination, with further subdivisions into instruction inconsistency, context inconsistency, and logical inconsistency (Alansari & Luqman, 2025). Both taxonomies acknowledge that different tasks carry different truth expectations, but neither parameterizes the boundary as a function of the user's intent or prompt.
Claim-level decomposition and verification. FActScore (Min et al., 2023) pioneered atomic fact decomposition: each model response is broken into minimal factual claims, each verified against a knowledge source. SAFE (Wei et al., 2024) automates this pipeline using GPT-4 for claim-wise verification. VeriFastScore (Rishanth & Miyyer, 2025) achieves a 6.6× speedup over VeriScore by performing joint claim decomposition and verification in a single pass, with Pearson \(r = 0.94\) system-level correlation with VeriScore across 12 LLMs (Rishanth & Miyyer, 2025). The Chinese Atomic Claim Decomposition Dataset (CACDD) provides 500 annotated question-answer pairs with 4,956 atomic claims, demonstrating that claim decomposition remains highly challenging even for state-of-the-art models (Zhang et al., 2024). All these systems treat each claim as binary (supported/unsupported) without conditioning the judgment on the expected regime.
Uncertainty-based detection. Kossen et al. (2024) introduce semantic entropy, computed over the meanings of sampled responses rather than lexical tokens, achieving 0.790 AUROC averaged across 30 task-model combinations — outperforming naive entropy (0.691), P(True) (0.698), and embedding regression (0.687) (Kossen et al., 2024). Semantic entropy focuses on "confabulations" — fluent but arbitrary claims sensitive to irrelevant details — providing a principled distinction from consistent errors. The semantic clustering step (bidirectional entailment) implicitly separates \(\sigma\) variation (different wordings of the same meaning cluster together) from \(\kappa\) variation (different factual claims form separate clusters).
Preemptive detection. Alnuhait et al. (2024) introduce FactCheckMate, which predicts whether an LLM will hallucinate from its hidden states before decoding begins, achieving over 70% preemptive detection accuracy. When hallucination is detected, FactCheckMate adjusts hidden states to produce 34.4% more factual outputs on average, evaluated across Llama, Mistral, Qwen, and Gemma families (Alnuhait et al., 2024). This approach leverages the internal truth representations identified by Marks & Tegmark (2024) for practical mitigation.
3.2 Sensitivity to Prompt-Dependent Truth Requirements (RQ-C2)
The extent to which existing detection methods account for prompt-dependent truth requirements is limited and typically implicit.
FAITHQA and Intent Constraints. Hao et al. (2025) introduce FAITHQA, a benchmark of 20,068 queries designed to evaluate intent hallucination — failures to satisfy the intent behind a query, beyond mere factual errors. The accompanying Intent Constraint metric decomposes user intents into checkable constraints, with human evaluation showing that Intent Constraint is closer to human judgment for intent hallucination than existing baselines (Hao et al., 2025). This is the closest existing work to regime-conditioned evaluation, as it moves beyond factual verification to assess whether the model's output satisfies the query's purpose. However, it does not formalize the intent as a regime with separable \(\sigma\) and \(\kappa\) components.
HalluLens. Bang et al. (2025) introduce HalluLens, a comprehensive benchmark that disentangles hallucination from factuality by proposing a clear taxonomy distinguishing extrinsic hallucination (inconsistency with training data) from intrinsic hallucination. HalluLens includes dynamic test set generation to prevent data leakage and addresses saturation of existing benchmarks (Bang et al., 2025). The explicit separation of hallucination from factuality error is an important conceptual step — it acknowledges that not all incorrect outputs result from the same mechanism — but the benchmark does not condition error definitions on the task or prompt.
Rubric-based approaches. Datadog's hallucination detection system uses a rubric-based LLM-as-judge approach that distinguishes contradictions (claims directly opposing the context) from unsupported claims (claims not grounded in the provided context) (Datadog, 2025). Crucially, the system allows configurable sensitivity: in high-stakes scenarios, both contradictions and unsupported claims are flagged, while in lower-stakes scenarios, only contradictions trigger alerts. This binary sensitivity switch is the simplest form of regime conditioning — a coarse-grained \(\kappa\) toggle — but it is manually configured rather than inferred from the prompt.
Reference-free detection. HalluJudge (Tantithamthavorn et al., 2025) introduces reference-free hallucination detection for code review automation, assessing context alignment between LLM-generated review comments and code diffs on a 5-point scale. Evaluated on Atlassian enterprise projects, it achieves 0.85 F1 with Tree-of-Thoughts prompting (Tantithamthavorn et al., 2025). The context-alignment formulation is inherently regime-aware in a narrow sense: the "truth" is defined by the code diff (a specific oracle \(O\)), and any departure is flagged. However, the approach does not account for situations where creative suggestions beyond the diff might be valuable.
3.3 Benchmarks and the Hallucination-vs-Acceptable-Divergence Boundary (RQ-C3)
Existing benchmarks overwhelmingly define a fixed ground truth and measure all departures as error, leaving the acceptable-divergence boundary unexamined. This section evaluates the extent to which current benchmarks could accommodate regime-dependent evaluation and identifies what would need to change.
OpenFactCheck. Iqbal et al. (2024) present OpenFactCheck, an open-source framework unifying factuality evaluation across three modules: ResponseEval (claim-level verification), LLMEval (model-level assessment across 6,480 examples), and CheckerEval (fact-checker benchmarking). The framework supports multiple back-end systems (RARR, FacTool, Factcheck-GPT) and evaluates across multiple error types: knowledge errors, over-commitment, and fast-changing information (Iqbal et al., 2024). The modular architecture and YAML-based configurability make OpenFactCheck a promising backbone for regime-aware evaluation: in principle, a regime-inference module could precede claim processing, and the verification criteria could be parameterized by \(\kappa\) (e.g., treating unsupported but plausible claims as acceptable under high-\(\kappa\) regimes). However, the current implementation assumes a fixed factual ground truth across all query types.
The inevitability argument. Xu et al. (2025) prove formally that hallucination is inevitable for LLMs used as general problem solvers, drawing on results from learning theory to show that LLMs cannot learn all computable functions. For real-world LLMs constrained by provable time complexity, they describe hallucination-prone tasks and discuss mechanisms of existing mitigators (Xu et al., 2025). This result has profound implications for the regime-aware approach: if hallucination cannot be eliminated, the only path forward is to manage it — deciding which hallucinations are acceptable (PD under permissive \(\kappa\)) and which are harmful (violations under restrictive \(\kappa\)). The inevitability result also implies that any benchmark aiming for zero hallucination is measuring the wrong objective; regime-aware benchmarks that measure appropriate hallucination rates conditional on task and intent would be both more realistic and more informative.
Creativity–hallucination boundary. Alansari & Luqman (2025) explicitly note a "gray zone" where hallucinatory outputs can be valued in creative writing or ideation tasks, acknowledging that creativity does not inherently violate accuracy while hallucination does (Alansari & Luqman, 2025). However, this acknowledgment is conceptual rather than operational — no benchmark or metric is proposed to formalize the boundary. The position paper's empirical study, with 12,469 texts crossing multiple formats and creative intents, represents the first benchmark that systematically varies both CI and format to measure their effects on hallucination rates, thereby operationalizing the creativity–hallucination boundary as a function of regime parameters.
3.4 Barriers to Claim-Level Regime-Conditioned Detection (RQ-C4)
Several technical and conceptual barriers impede the development of claim-level detection conditioned on truth regime.
Granularity vs. context loss. Atomic claim decomposition (FActScore, SAFE, VeriFastScore) provides the granularity needed for regime-conditioned evaluation, but decomposition strips claims of pragmatic context. A claim like "the capital of Narnia is Cair Paravel" is hallucination in a geography report and correct in a literary discussion — but the claim in isolation carries no regime information. VeriFastScore's claim-level evaluation achieves \(r = 0.87\) correlation with VeriScore but loses regime context by design (Rishanth & Miyyer, 2025).
Compositional truth values. The RELIANCE framework demonstrates that even with correct final answers, intermediate reasoning steps can contain factual inaccuracies. The fact-checking classifier achieves up to 97.20% accuracy on detecting errors in reasoning chains, and GRPO-based reinforcement learning with factuality rewards improves step-level factual accuracy by up to +49.90% (RELIANCE, 2025). However, this raises a regime question: if an intermediate step is factually wrong but heuristically useful (as in the brute-force programs of Zakhalili et al., 2025), should the RELIANCE classifier flag it?
Knowledge source limitations. Token-level hallucination detection via KCTS uses a knowledge classifier trained on synthetic data to guide decoding toward grounded text, achieving 98.30% groundedness on Wizard of Wikipedia with Monte-Carlo Tree Search (KCTS, 2023). The RIPA labeling scheme (grounded=1 until first hallucination token, then 0) explicitly models the "hallucination snowballing" effect. However, the approach requires a reference knowledge source \(k\), which is equivalent to fixing the oracle \(O\) — it cannot accommodate regimes where the model is permitted to go beyond the provided knowledge.
Scalability of regime inference. All regime-conditioned approaches would require running inference on the regime itself before or during generation. Dang et al.'s (2025) PS/MV framework shows that prompt-model interactions can amplify hallucination risk (Joint Attribution Score up to 0.12 for ambiguous prompts on susceptible models), suggesting that regime inference must account for model-specific sensitivities (Dang et al., 2025).
3.5 Toward Integrated Regime-Aware Pipelines (RQ-C5)
No existing system fully integrates truth-regime inference with detection and mitigation, but several works point toward components of such a pipeline.
Regime specification through prompting. Zhou et al.'s context-faithful prompting (Zhou et al., 2023) and Li et al.'s finding that instructional prompts reduce HFH by ~50% (Li et al., 2026) demonstrate that explicit regime specification in the prompt is effective. A pipeline could incorporate automatic regime-to-prompt translation as its first stage.
Multi-component verification. OpenFactCheck's modular architecture (claim processing → retrieval → verification) provides an extensible backbone for regime-aware evaluation. By inserting a regime-inference module before claim processing, the verification criteria could be conditioned on the inferred \(\text{TR}(p)\) (Iqbal et al., 2024).
Decoding-time control. KCTS demonstrates that hallucination can be controlled at the token level during decoding via knowledge-constrained search (KCTS, 2023), while Top-H decoding shows that creativity can be controlled via entropy constraints (Potraghloo et al., 2025). A regime-aware decoding system could switch between these strategies based on the inferred \(\kappa\): low \(\kappa\) → KCTS-style knowledge-constrained decoding; high \(\kappa\) → Top-H-style entropy-managed exploration.
Internal representation monitoring. FactCheckMate's preemptive detection from hidden states (Alnuhait et al., 2024) combined with Marks & Tegmark's geometry of truth (Marks & Tegmark, 2024) suggests that regime violations could be detected in real time from model internals, enabling intervention before problematic content is generated. Semantic entropy (Kossen et al., 2024) provides a complementary signal: high semantic entropy under a low-\(\kappa\) regime indicates the model is confabulating, whereas the same entropy under a high-\(\kappa\) regime indicates exploration.
Reinforcement learning for regime compliance. RELIANCE's GRPO framework with a factuality reward \(R_\text{fact}\) demonstrates that reinforcement learning can steer models toward factual intermediate reasoning without collapsing overall performance (RELIANCE, 2025). Extending this to regime-aware rewards — where \(R_\text{fact}\) is weighted by the inferred \(\kappa\) — could produce models that are strict where required and creative where permitted.
3.6 Positioning Relative to the Position Paper
The position paper's contribution to Axis C is the explicit proposal to replace binary detection with regime-conditioned evaluation, where a claim is hallucination if and only if it violates the operative \(\text{TR}(p)\). The literature confirms that the necessary building blocks exist — claim-level decomposition, uncertainty-based detection, knowledge-constrained decoding, internal representation probing — but they have never been assembled into a system that conditions its behavior on the truth regime. The empirical finding that 310K claims across 10 models and 12,469 texts show format as the dominant driver of hallucination rate provides the quantitative foundation for regime-aware benchmarks: different formats instantiate different \((\sigma, \kappa)\) pairs, and evaluation must respect these differences. The gap between current binary evaluation and regime-conditioned evaluation defines the central technical contribution of the thesis in this axis.


4. Cross-Axis Synthesis and Research Gaps
4.1 What the Literature Covers Well
The reviewed literature provides strong foundations in several areas, each representing decades of cumulative work that the thesis can build upon rather than replicate:

Hallucination taxonomy and claim-level verification. The progression from Ji et al.'s (2023) document-level taxonomy through FActScore's atomic decomposition to VeriFastScore's efficient single-pass evaluation represents a mature pipeline for binary factual checking.
Creativity–hallucination tradeoff quantification. The DAT benchmark, CreativityPrism, LiveIdeaBench, and the HCL layer-wise analysis collectively demonstrate that the tradeoff is measurable, model-dependent, and controllable through decoding strategy and temperature.
Ambiguity detection and clarification. CAMBIGNQ, ProCoT, and domain-specific clarification systems establish that LLMs can be prompted to detect ambiguity and ask clarifying questions, though performance remains limited.
Internal truth representations. The geometry of truth (Marks & Tegmark), FactCheckMate's preemptive detection, and semantic entropy provide convergent evidence that LLMs encode truth-relevant information in their activations, enabling detection without external knowledge sources.
4.2 Identified Gaps Across All Three Axes
Despite this progress, six critical gaps remain that collectively define the opportunity for the thesis. These gaps are not merely quantitative (more data, better models) but structural (missing formalisms, absent conditioning mechanisms):

No formalized truth-regime construct. No prior work defines a structured representation \(\text{TR}(p) = (O, \sigma, \kappa)\) that decomposes the expected truth contract into oracle, stylistic latitude, and factual invention permission. Existing intent taxonomies, prompt clarity scores, and instruction compliance metrics each capture a fragment but lack the formal unification needed for downstream conditioning.
No \(\sigma\)/\(\kappa\) decomposition in evaluation. All surveyed benchmarks and metrics treat departures from ground truth monolithically. No benchmark systematically varies stylistic freedom and factual permission to measure their independent and interactive effects on hallucination rates — except the position paper's own empirical study.
No regime-conditioned detection pipeline. Despite the availability of claim-level decomposition, uncertainty estimation, and knowledge-constrained decoding, no existing system conditions its detection threshold or mitigation strategy on an inferred truth regime. The Datadog rubric system's manual sensitivity toggle is the closest approximation.
Limited cross-domain evidence for productive divergence. While code and mathematics offer formal verification, evidence for PD in less formal domains (design, brainstorming, strategic reasoning) relies on creativity metrics that do not verify factual correctness. The boundary between PD and H in these domains remains unformalized.
Ambiguity-to-regime mapping. Existing clarification systems ask about referential ambiguity ("which entity?") rather than regime ambiguity ("do you want facts or speculation?"). No system maps detected ambiguity onto truth-regime parameters.
Interaction between regime inference and generation. The literature treats intent classification, generation, and evaluation as separate problems. An end-to-end system that infers the regime, conditions generation on it, and evaluates outputs against it does not exist.
4.3 Implications for the Thesis Research Program
The identified gaps map directly onto the six open problems proposed in the position paper:

Open Problem	Corresponding Gap	Priority Axes
Operationalizing CI/TR as scorable variables	No formalized TR construct; no \(\sigma\)/\(\kappa\) decomposition	A, C
Automatic TR inference from prompts	Ambiguity-to-regime mapping absent; intent taxonomies lack regime parameters	A

The thesis research program is uniquely positioned to bridge these axes because the \(\text{TR}(p) = (O, \sigma, \kappa)\) formalization provides the shared vocabulary that current approaches lack. Axis A (regime inference) feeds the conditioning variable; Axis B (productive divergence) defines the permissible space under high-\(\kappa\) regimes; Axis C (detection and mitigation) consumes the inferred regime to modulate its behavior. The resulting system would be the first to evaluate LLM outputs not against a fixed ground truth but against the truth contract implied by the interaction.
The practical implications extend beyond evaluation. A regime-aware system could adaptively control generation — applying KCTS-style knowledge constraints under low-\(\kappa\) regimes and Top-H-style entropy management under high-\(\kappa\) regimes, while using semantic entropy to monitor the boundary in real time. The explore-then-verify paradigm documented in code and mathematics (Tang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2025) could be generalized to any task where the regime permits intermediate speculation, provided convergence mechanisms are available. Most importantly, the \(\sigma\)/\(\kappa\) decomposition would enable fair evaluation of LLMs: a model that produces creative paraphrases (high \(\sigma\), low \(\kappa\)) would no longer be penalized by benchmarks that confuse stylistic variation with factual invention.
Three immediate priorities emerge from this review. First, a large-scale annotation study mapping prompts to \(\text{TR}(p)\) tuples would provide the training data needed for automatic regime inference — building on the intent taxonomies of Bodonhelyi et al. and Shah et al. but extending from coarse categories to continuous \((\sigma, \kappa)\) parameters. Second, extending the position paper's 12,469-text empirical study with explicit convergence mechanisms (as in ECP and the code repair framework) would test whether regime-aware generation improves outcomes in formal domains. Third, integrating VeriFastScore's efficient claim decomposition with a regime inference module would yield the first prototype of a regime-conditioned evaluation pipeline, directly addressing the central gap identified across all three axes.

This literature review confirms that the conceptual and technical components for a regime-aware approach to LLM evaluation exist in fragmentary form across the three axes. Intent classification, creativity benchmarking, claim-level decomposition, uncertainty estimation, knowledge-constrained decoding, and internal truth probing have each been developed in isolation. The unifying contribution of the truth-regime framework — conditioning detection, evaluation, and mitigation on a formalized \(\text{TR}(p) = (O, \sigma, \kappa)\) — remains an open and well-motivated research agenda that the thesis is positioned to advance.

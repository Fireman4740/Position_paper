\begin{abstract}
Hallucination is usually scored as a binary defect: a statement is supported or it is not.
This misses a central property of language use: the same unsupported content can be dangerous in a medical answer, harmless in a metaphor, and useful in fiction or ideation.
We argue that hallucination should instead be evaluated relative to the prompt's \emph{truth regime}, $\TR(p)=(O,\sigma,\kappa)$: the oracle $O$ that grounds truth, the stylistic latitude $\sigma$ that permits surface variation, and the invention license $\kappa$ that determines whether new truth-conditional content may be introduced.
Under this view, hallucination (\Hallu) is a truth-regime violation, whereas productive divergence (\PD) is divergence that the regime licenses and the task rewards.
We then show that current hallucination and creativity benchmarks share a blind spot: neither conditions its scores on what the prompt authorizes.
We substantiate this gap by an illustrative audit of representative benchmarks against $(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ and by applying a claim-level labeling rule to documented cases in which the same output changes status as the regime changes.
These observations motivate a research agenda for truth-regime-aware evaluation that scores \Hallu\ and \PD\ separately under explicit oracle and permission annotations.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction: The Creativity--Hallucination Paradox}\label{sec:intro}

Large language models (LLMs) sometimes state things that are not true.
The community calls these errors \emph{hallucinations}: the model produces fluent, confident text that is not supported by evidence or contradicts known facts \citep{ji_survey_2023, huang_survey_2025}.
Hallucination is widely regarded as the central reliability barrier for deploying LLMs in high-stakes settings such as medicine, law, and education \citep{lin_truthfulqa_2022}.
A large body of work consequently aims to detect, measure, and reduce it \citep{li_halueval_2023, wei_measuring_2024, thorne_fever_2018}.

A paradox sits underneath this effort.
When users ask an LLM to write fiction, brainstorm, or propose creative solutions, they actively \emph{want} the model to go beyond established facts, and punishing every unsupported statement would suppress the very outputs they seek \citep{franceschelli_creativity_2024, hou_creativityprism_2025, rick_supermind_2023}.
A brainstorming tool that only repeats ideas that already exist is useless; a medical assistant that invents diagnoses is dangerous.
The difference lies not in the text itself, but in what the prompt asked for.

\paragraph{The diagnostic gap.}
Current evaluation practice does not register this difference.
Hallucination benchmarks treat any unsupported content as a defect to minimize \citep{bang_hallulens_2025}, whereas creativity benchmarks treat novelty as a virtue to maximize, often without checking factual grounding \citep{hou_creativityprism_2025, acar_creativity_2019}.
Neither tradition conditions its scores on what the user's prompt actually authorizes.
As a result, one output can be scored as a serious failure by one tradition and a creative success by another, depending on which protocol is applied rather than on whether the divergence was appropriate.

Even the closest recent steps stop short of the contract.
Intent-aware evaluation decomposes a query into checkable constraints, but those constraints cover query elements (subject, action, quantity) rather than stylistic latitude or invention license, and it scores intent coverage with no truth oracle: it has neither $O$ nor $\kappa$ \citep{hao_faithqa_2025}.
The closest analogue to our distinction splits hallucinations into ``intelligent'' and ``defective,'' yet it reads that label off the output's own creativity and factual-deviation scores rather than off the prompt, so the label is fixed by the text and cannot flip with the contract that should govern it \citep{yang_hicbench_2025}.
Neither makes $(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ a scored variable, and that omission is the gap we identify.

\paragraph{What the contract is not.}
$(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ is not a relabeling of grounding, style, or task type.
$O$ fixes what counts as support but cannot say whether leaving the evidence is licensed.
$\sigma$ governs form but cannot license new truth-conditional content.
A task label is too coarse because the same task can pair high stylistic freedom with $\kappa{=}0$, or neutral form with $\kappa{>}0$.
The irreducible component is $\kappa$: it determines whether unsupported content is an error, a flagged hypothesis, or invention inside a declared frame.

Better detectors alone do not address the missing variable, which is not only evidential support but authorization: whether the task licensed the model to leave the evidence.
Recent theoretical arguments moreover suggest that unsupported generation will not disappear under broad task distributions: appeals to incompleteness and undecidability \citep{banerjee_llms_2024, xu_hallucination_2024} posit a nonzero structural error floor.
Yet the same generative mechanism also produces the novel hypotheses and creative combinations that users request in open-ended tasks \citep{sun_hallucinating_2024, he_shakespearean_2025}.
The goal, therefore, cannot be to eliminate divergence, but to judge it against what the task permits.

\paragraph{Our thesis.}
Hallucination is not a binary property of text.
It is \emph{relative to the prompt's truth regime} (\TR): the task-specific contract $\TR(p)=(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ specifying the oracle $O$ against which claims are checked, the stylistic latitude $\sigma$ granted to the form of the output, and the invention license $\kappa$ that determines whether new truth-conditional content may be introduced.
Under a strict \TR\ (for example, ``summarize this document accurately''), any unsupported claim is a hallucination (\Hallu).
Under a permissive \TR\ (for example, ``write fiction''), that same divergence may instead constitute \emph{productive divergence} (\PD): a regime-compliant departure from established facts that is novel and task-useful, and therefore fulfills rather than violates what the prompt requested.
The distinction turns not on the text but on whether the prompt authorized the divergence; evaluation that ignores this conflates \Hallu\ with \PD.
In practice, the distinction is already actionable: an evaluator who annotates a prompt's oracle and invention license can withhold the hallucination penalty wherever the regime authorized the divergence, scoring it as \PD\ rather than as error, a judgment no regime-agnostic scorer can make.
This is also what separates \TR\ from context-faithfulness: faithfulness asks only whether an output stays within its evidence, and has no analogue of $\kappa$, the explicit license to leave it.

\paragraph{How we establish the gap.}
We argue this position rather than establish it with a new controlled study.
Two analytical moves carry the weight.
First, we present an illustrative audit of representative hallucination and creativity benchmarks against the three components $(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ and find that, while the oracle is sometimes fixed, stylistic latitude and invention license are essentially never annotated, so the contract remains implicit (Section~\ref{sec:sota}).
Second, we apply a claim-level labeling rule to documented cases and show that the same output receives opposite verdicts (\Hallu\ under one regime, \PD\ under another) once the contract is made explicit (Section~\ref{sec:worked}).
Together these suggest the gap is not merely anecdotal: it follows from leaving the prompt's truth commitments implicit, and a lightweight layer making $(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ explicit would resolve verdicts that current pipelines leave underdetermined.
Preliminary observations of our own are consistent with this analysis; we report them only as motivation (Section~\ref{sec:sota}) and defer a controlled, regime-aware study to future empirical work.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This position paper makes three contributions.
\emph{First} (conceptual), we introduce $\TR(p)=(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ and a minimal vocabulary (\TR, \PD, \Hallu) that makes explicit what the prompt authorizes, separating the two components that bear on truth (the oracle $O$ and the invention license $\kappa$) from the stylistic latitude $\sigma$ that governs form and conditions measurement validity (Section~\ref{sec:framework}).
\emph{Second} (diagnostic), we show that hallucination and creativity evaluation share a blind spot (neither conditions its scores on the truth regime), and we demonstrate it concretely by auditing existing benchmarks and by applying the labeling rule to documented cases (Sections~\ref{sec:sota}~and~\ref{sec:worked}).
\emph{Third} (programmatic), we outline a research agenda for \TR-aware benchmarks that score \Hallu\ and \PD\ separately under explicit oracle and permission annotations (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}).


\section{Terminology and Conceptual Framework}\label{sec:framework}

\paragraph{Motivation.}
The literature uses the word \emph{hallucination} broadly, but not consistently.
Surveys commonly group together factuality errors against world knowledge and faithfulness errors against a provided source, sometimes adding axes such as intrinsic versus extrinsic causes and different granularities \citep{ji_survey_2023, huang_survey_2025}.
Benchmarking work further argues that hallucination is often conflated with factuality and that any verdict depends on the chosen oracle and on evaluator reliability \citep{bang_hallulens_2025}.
To reason about creativity--hallucination trade-offs, we fix a minimal vocabulary that makes the prompt's \emph{truth regime} explicit.
Throughout, we separate \emph{prompt-level} variables (what the user requests) from \emph{output-level} properties (what the model produces), and adopt a \emph{claim-level} unit of analysis because evaluations vary by granularity.

\textbf{Truth regime (\TR; prompt-level contract).}
A task-specific contract $\TR(p)=(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ that determines what must be \emph{grounded}, how freely the \emph{form} may vary, and what may be \emph{invented}.
\TR\ is needed because both the definition of ``hallucination'' and the evaluation oracle shift across settings \citep{bang_hallulens_2025}.
We treat $\TR(p)$ as an \emph{idealization}: in deployment the contract is shaped not only by the visible prompt but also by system instructions, domain conventions, and user expectations, and an ambiguous prompt may underdetermine it.
Inferring \TR\ under such ambiguity is itself an open problem (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}); we nonetheless locate the contract at the prompt because that is where evaluation can render it explicit and auditable.

\textbf{Oracle ($O$).}
The reference against which truth-conditional claims are checked: a source document, a retrieval set, a database, world knowledge, or user-provided facts.
The same claim can be supported by one oracle and unsupported by another, so $O$ must be specified rather than assumed \citep{bang_hallulens_2025, ji_survey_2023}.

\textbf{Stylistic latitude ($\sigma$).}
How freely the \emph{form} of the output may vary while preserving its propositions.
This axis is studied independently of content: style is not a single variable but a bundle of co-varying dimensions \citep{kang_style_2021}, it can be transferred while meaning is held fixed \citep{jin_deep_2022}, and the dual operation (moving facts across a fixed style) is equally well defined \citep{balepur_text_2023}.
Figurative devices such as metaphor and hyperbole are formal deviations that leave truth conditions intact \citep{badathala_match_2023}, and foregrounding, the long-standing notion that stylistically marked language stands out against an unmarked background \citep{leech_short_style_1981}, is reliably recognized by readers \citep{vanpeer_stylistics_2020}, which is what lets a graded $\sigma$ be annotated rather than guessed; established formality datasets and style-transfer metrics make the axis operationally measurable \citep{pavlick_empirical_2016, rao_dear_2018, briakou_evaluating_2021}.
For now we treat $\sigma$ as a coarse ``low''/``high'' distinction: low is literal, neutral form (no marked figures or authorial voice); high is strongly marked form (sustained imagery, salient voice).
We keep this scale binary deliberately. A finer, validated gradation of stylistic latitude (and in particular a decision about where the contested ``moderately marked'' middle falls) calls for input from stylistics that is still ongoing; we therefore defer a graded $\sigma$ to future work (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}).
By construction, $\sigma$ licenses variation in form only: a metaphor that preserves the underlying proposition introduces no new truth-conditional content.
Two consequences fix $\sigma$'s role in the framework.
First, $\sigma$ is orthogonal to truth: since it neither adds nor removes a proposition, it cannot by itself make a claim \Hallu\ or \PD, and varying it leaves both faithfulness (\Faith) and accuracy (\Acc) unchanged in principle; those verdicts turn only on $O$ and $\kappa$, the two components that bear on content.
Second, $\sigma$ is nonetheless load-bearing for \emph{measurement}: raising stylistic latitude is exactly what leads an automatic verifier to misread marked form as unsupported content, so a regime that leaves $\sigma$ unspecified leaves the validity of any \Hallu\ count undefined.
$\sigma$ thus enters the contract not as a third verdict axis but as the dimension along which measurement of the other two must be held invariant (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}).

\textbf{Invention license ($\kappa$).}
Whether new truth-conditional content may be introduced.
We use three levels: $\kappa{=}0$, no invention (every truth-conditional claim must be supported by $O$); $\kappa{=}1$, flagged speculation (hypotheses and extrapolations are permitted provided they are marked as such); $\kappa{=}2$, free invention within a declared frame (fiction, ideation), bound by coherence and user-provided facts rather than by $O$.
Theoretically, $\kappa$ tracks the kind of speech act the prompt licenses: at $\kappa{=}0$ the model must make genuine assertions, committing to the truth of what it adds to the common ground \citep{stalnaker_assertion_1978}, whereas a declared fictional frame ($\kappa{=}2$) licenses what \citet{searle_logical_1975} calls pretended assertions, utterances with the form of assertion but without the commitment to truth, with $\kappa{=}1$ in between, admitting assertions explicitly marked as provisional.
The two permissions are independent: many real prompts request creative style without factual invention (for example, ``explain photosynthesis like a poet''), so $\sigma$ is high while $\kappa{=}0$, and figurative language is welcome but fabricated claims are not \citep{franceschelli_creativity_2024}.
Throughout, we write $\sigma$ ``low''/``high'' for the bottom/top of its scale, and refer to $\kappa$ by its level ($0$, $1$, or $2$).

\textbf{Faithfulness (\Faith; output-level).}
Whether the output is \emph{supported by the provided evidence} (documents, retrieved passages, tables), without introducing unsupported details.
This is the central failure mode in grounded generation and summarization, where fluent outputs can exceed source support \citep{maynez_faithfulness_2020, kryscinski_evaluating_2020}.

\textbf{Accuracy / factuality (\Acc; output-level).}
Whether the claims that must be true under \TR\ are actually supported by an appropriate external source (for example, references, gold labels, or world knowledge).
This corresponds to question answering (QA) and fact-checking evaluation.

\textbf{Hallucination (\Hallu; claim-level).}
As we define it, a hallucination is a \TR-violation: the model presents a claim \emph{as grounded/true} where \TR\ requires truth or evidence, but the claim is unsupported (\Faith) and/or incorrect (\Acc).
This unifies faithfulness and factuality failures while making the oracle explicit \citep{maynez_faithfulness_2020, bang_hallulens_2025}.

\textbf{Productive divergence (\PD; claim-level).}
Content that would count as hallucination under a strict factual standard, but is allowed by the prompt's truth regime and helps accomplish the task.
A claim is \PD\ when three conditions hold: it is \emph{licensed} (the regime permits invention, $\kappa>0$, or the claim falls inside a declared fictional frame); it is \emph{useful} (novel and appropriate under the task constraints, the standard creativity criterion \citep{diedrich_are_2015, acar_creativity_2019}); and it is \emph{presented on the regime's terms} (a flagged hypothesis under $\kappa{=}1$ must not be asserted as verified fact).
The construct is therefore decomposable rather than fully solved: licensing and stance are auditable gates, while usefulness is the soft component that must be reported with disagreement rather than hidden inside a binary label.
This echoes non-LLM uses of ``productive divergence'' as variation that energizes performance \citep{parry_team_2023, lingard_pulling_2017}.
A contrast pins the category down.
Given ``summarize this medical report,'' an added claim that the patient has diabetes, absent from the report, violates a regime where $O$ is the report and $\kappa{=}0$: it is \Hallu.
Given ``write a poem about quantum physics,'' the line that electrons dream in parallel worlds is supported by no oracle, yet the regime licenses figurative invention: it is \PD\ if it serves the poem.
Both statements are invented; only the first is a failure.
If \TR\ demands grounding, the same divergence is \Hallu, not \PD.

\begin{tcolorbox}[enhanced, colback=clrPD!5, colframe=clrPD!45, boxrule=0.35pt, arc=2pt,
  left=6pt, right=6pt, top=3pt, bottom=3pt, before skip=4pt, after skip=4pt]
\footnotesize\textbf{\PD\ is not a binary ground-truth label.}
It is a structured judgment with three parts: \emph{license-pass} ($\kappa>0$ and the claim is in scope), \emph{stance-pass} (marked as the regime requires, not asserted as verified fact), and a \emph{usefulness} score.
The first two are constraint-like and checkable; the third is evaluator-dependent and should be reported with its uncertainty or annotator disagreement rather than collapsed into one label (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}).
\end{tcolorbox}

\noindent\textbf{Decision rule.}
Given a prompt $p$, an output $y$, and an oracle $O$: specify $\TR(p)$, decompose $y$ into atomic claims, and label each claim in three layers.
First record its \emph{evidence state} against $O$ (supported, unsupported, or unverifiable).
A truth-conditional claim not entailed by $O$ (whether unsupported or merely unverifiable) is not yet a verdict: it is \Hallu\ when the departure is unlicensed ($\kappa{=}0$ or outside the licensed scope) or is asserted as verified fact where the license required a hedge, and \PD\ when the regime licenses it, it is presented on the regime's terms, and it is task-useful; a supported claim, or purely stylistic variation, is neither.
Figure~\ref{fig:annotation-tree} renders the procedure as a decision tree; Appendix~\ref{app:decision-rule} gives the step-by-step version.
The evidence state is mechanically checkable against $O$; the usefulness condition inherits the evaluator dependence of creativity judgments (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}).

\paragraph{Worked example.}
Consider ``Explain photosynthesis in a poetic style, but keep the science accurate.''
Here the oracle is scientific world knowledge, $\sigma$ high because figurative language is requested, and $\kappa{=}0$ because the science must stay accurate.
Calling chlorophyll ``a green workshop for sunlight'' is stylistic variation, but claiming that plants make sugar from moonlight is \Hallu.
If the prompt instead asks for a fictional plant ecology where moonlight powers growth, the same moonlight claim can be \PD: useful, clearly framed as invented, and consistent with the fictional constraints.

\noindent Under this vocabulary we do not oppose ``creativity versus truth.''
We oppose \textbf{\Hallu} (\TR\ violations) to \textbf{\PD} (\TR-compliant creativity); regime-agnostic factuality evaluation fails to separate the two.

\paragraph{Why $\kappa$ is not reducible to faithfulness.}
Faithfulness asks whether an output stays within its evidence; $\kappa$ asks whether leaving it is licensed and, when it is, with what commitment the content must be presented.
The two come apart at $\kappa{=}1$.
Consider a clinical triage prompt whose regime requests flagged speculation (``list possible causes, and mark anything not yet established'').
Two outputs carry the \emph{same} proposition.
One states it flatly, ``this is early Lyme disease''; the other qualifies it, ``this could be early Lyme disease, but it is speculative and needs serology.''
A faithfulness or factuality verifier scores the proposition, not its epistemic marking: against an oracle that cannot yet confirm the cause, both are unsupported, so both are flagged alike.
The regime separates them.
The flat assertion is a \Hallu, a \emph{confident error} stated as fact where the license required a hedge, whereas the qualified version is \PD: licensed speculation, presented on the regime's terms.
This is the \emph{faithful uncertainty} that \citet{yona_metacognition_2026} argue separates a trustworthy answer from a hallucinated one, aligning a claim's linguistic uncertainty with what the model actually knows.
The license $\kappa$ makes that alignment a scored term of the contract; faithfulness has no slot for it, which is why no faithfulness metric can recover the verdict $\kappa$ fixes.

\paragraph{Scope and minimality of the contract.}
We claim $(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ is a \emph{sufficient} basis for the verdict (enough to flip the verdicts current pipelines get wrong), rather than a complete model of everything evaluation must weigh.
One axis is deliberately left out: \emph{severity}.
A medical question and a trivia question can share an identical contract ($O=$ world knowledge, $\sigma$ low, $\kappa{=}0$), yet a fabricated drug dose and a wrong trivia date are not equally costly.
Severity differs, but the verdict does not: both fabrications are \Hallu.
Severity therefore weights the penalty on a violation (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}); it does not decide whether a claim violates the regime, so it belongs to the scoring layer above $(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ rather than inside it.
Other plausible factors reduce to the three components instead of extending them: audience and genre act on evaluation through the oracle they select and the latitude they grant.
We make no completeness claim.
$(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ is the smallest set that already suffices to flip the verdicts current pipelines get wrong, and enlarging it stays open.

\subsection{Truth regimes and regime-relative divergence}\label{sec:truth-regimes}

The components of $\TR(p)=(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ combine into recognizable task profiles \citep{ji_survey_2023,huang_survey_2025}.
Figure~\ref{fig:sk-map} places the task profiles in the $(\sigma,\kappa)$ plane: three common regimes and one uncommon corner.
Existing benchmarks typically fix $O$ but rarely annotate $(\sigma,\kappa)$ explicitly, making it easy to conflate \PD\ with \Hallu\ \citep{bang_hallulens_2025}.

\paragraph{Claim-level labeling.}
Given a regime $\TR(p)$, each atomic claim is labelled \emph{stylistic variation} (form changes, no new propositions), \emph{supported} (entailed by $O$), \Hallu\ (an unlicensed or mis-presented truth commitment), or \PD\ (licensed, presented on the regime's terms, and task-useful) \citep{ji_survey_2023,bang_hallulens_2025}.
A minor truth-conditional inaccuracy is not a separate ``benign'' category but a low-severity \Hallu, weighted in the scoring layer rather than excused (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}); only licensed divergence that adds nothing useful is recorded as benign.
\Hallu\ and \PD\ are the two scored verdicts; the remaining labels are the non-divergent or negligible outcomes.

\noindent\textbf{Two-sided failure relative to $\kappa$.}
When $\kappa{=}0$, excess divergence increases \Hallu; when $\kappa{>}0$, \emph{insufficient} divergence yields conservative outputs that fail the creative objective, a failure mode captured by creativity benchmarks but invisible to hallucination-only scoring \citep{hou_creativityprism_2025}.
The two sides sit at different levels: excess divergence is visible claim by claim, whereas insufficient divergence is an output-level absence, a distinction we make precise when defining verdicts (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).
A regime-aware evaluator must therefore be able to fail an output in either direction, which a single unsupportedness count cannot.

With this vocabulary and labeling procedure in place, the natural question is whether existing benchmarks already condition their scores on the truth regime.
The next section examines both hallucination and creativity benchmarks and shows that they do not.

\section{The Diagnostic Gap: Evaluation That Ignores the Truth Regime}\label{sec:sota}

\paragraph{Two traditions, one shared blind spot.}
Hallucination benchmarks treat divergence from an oracle as a defect to minimize \citep{ji_survey_2023, bang_hallulens_2025}; creativity benchmarks treat it as evidence of novelty to maximize \citep{hou_creativityprism_2025}.
The split works for single-intent tasks but breaks down for mixed-intent prompts that pair novelty with factual constraints \citep{wang_user-centric_2024}: the same surface divergence can be \PD\ or \Hallu, yet on both sides the truth regime $(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ stays implicit.
Table~\ref{tab:audit} documents the omission.
Auditing a representative sample of forty benchmarks, organized by \emph{what} they evaluate (hallucination/faithfulness versus creativity/divergence) and by the \emph{task} they score, we find the same pattern throughout: the oracle $O$ is frequently fixed, but stylistic latitude $\sigma$ is never annotated and the invention license $\kappa$ is never graded.
Hallucination benchmarks inherit a fixed $O$ and run at $\sigma$ low and $\kappa{=}0$ (neutral form, no invention); creativity benchmarks drop the factual oracle and run at $\kappa{=}2$, with $\sigma$ high in the writing suites; the few intent-aware benchmarks condition on the query without separating $\sigma$ from $\kappa$.
The contract is left to the task label.

\paragraph{What the columns show, and what they do not.}
The $\sigma$ and $\kappa$ entries are \emph{our} annotations, inferred from each task's design rather than reported by the benchmark: a factual-QA or summarization suite implies $\sigma$ low and $\kappa{=}0$ (neutral form, no invention), a creative-writing suite implies $\sigma$ high and $\kappa{=}2$, and an open-ended innovation suite implies $\kappa{=}2$.
We make this inference explicit precisely because no benchmark measures any of these levels itself.
The empirical object of the audit is the protocol-level absence of scored regime variables.
These entries are author annotations used to expose that absence; they are not measurements reported by the benchmark authors.
Read that way, the finding is not that the column entries repeat but that every one is read off the task rather than scored: where a task plainly demands stylistic latitude (creative-writing suites) we mark $\sigma$ high, and where invention is central (creativity benchmarks, and the intelligent-hallucination split of \textsc{HIC-Bench}) we mark $\kappa{=}2$, yet no benchmark in either tradition turns that latitude or that license into a scored variable.
The recurring pattern across this sample, not any single column, is that neither permission is measured.

\paragraph{The cell no benchmark fills.}
Annotated this way, the forty benchmarks cluster on a diagonal: hallucination suites at $\sigma$ low, $\kappa{=}0$ (neutral form, strict grounding) and creative-writing suites at $\sigma$ high, $\kappa{=}2$ (marked form, free invention).
The off-diagonal cell $\sigma$ high, $\kappa{=}0$ (vivid or figurative form under a strict factual regime) is filled by none of them, though Figure~\ref{fig:sk-map} marks it as a canonical regime (a factual task with creative style) and it names an everyday request (``explain this like a poet, but keep the science exact'').
That cell is not an incidental hole: it is exactly where stylistic latitude and the truth oracle pull against each other, and where an automatic verifier is most likely to misread marked form as unsupported content.
Worse, the only region where high $\sigma$ appears at all ($\sigma$ high, $\kappa{=}2$) is scored solely on creativity metrics, so no existing benchmark measures hallucination under high stylistic latitude.
Building a benchmark for this cell (where creative form meets a strict oracle) is therefore a concrete priority of our agenda (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}).

\paragraph{How the audit was coded.}
We count a benchmark as annotating $\sigma$ only when stylistic latitude is an explicit, scored variable in its protocol, not merely present in some prompts, and as grading $\kappa$ only when invention is a scored dimension with more than one licensed level.
By this criterion \textsc{WritingBench} \citep{wu_writingbench_2025} is the instructive near-miss: it scores how well an output matches a requested \emph{style}, yet it never treats stylistic latitude as a graded permission that conditions the truth measurement, so by our test $\sigma$ stays unannotated even though the task plainly implies it is high.
The coding is a single-author pass against these criteria, offered as the first instance of the annotation layer we advocate rather than as a validated resource; borderline cases remain (does \textsc{WritingBench} \emph{score style} or \emph{annotate stylistic latitude}?), and independent re-coding with reported agreement is itself part of the agenda (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}).

\paragraph{An indicative observation.}
In preliminary, exploratory runs that held the oracle fixed and licensed no invention ($\kappa{=}0$), raising a prompt's stylistic latitude $\sigma$ alone moved what a binary verifier flagged as unsupported.
In principle $\sigma$ changes no proposition and so leaves both faithfulness and accuracy untouched; any such movement is therefore a property of the verifier, not of the output's truth-status. This measurement brittleness is what our agenda targets (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}).
Because that verifier is binary and lacks a \PD\ label, we report these runs only as motivation and leave a controlled, regime-aware study to future empirical work.

\subsection{Hallucination evaluation assumes a strict regime}
Hallucination evaluation is anchored in the choice of an oracle, either \emph{world knowledge} (\Acc: factuality) or \emph{provided context} (\Faith: faithfulness), with further axes for contradiction versus fabrication and evaluation granularity \citep{ji_survey_2023, bang_hallulens_2025}.
Modern protocols rely on claim-level decomposition and automated verification, for example, retrieval with natural language inference (NLI) \citep{chen_menli_2023} or QA-based checking \citep{wang_asking_2020}, building on fact-checking \citep{thorne_fever_2018}.
These pipelines are technically mature: atomic-claim decomposition is established \citep{min_factscore_2023}, joint decomposition and verification run at scale \citep{rajendhran_verifastscore_2025}, some verifiers add a third \texttt{undecidable} verdict \citep{bayat_factbench_2024}, and complementary signals such as semantic entropy \citep{farquhar_detecting_2024} or internal probes \citep{marks_geometry_2023} flag unsupported content without consulting any prompt-level contract.
Yet these axes are necessary, not sufficient, for \TR-aware judgment: most pipelines assume a strict invention license ($\kappa{=}0$), so any unsupported truth-conditional content is a failure.
The assumption is defensible in grounded generation but collapses when prompts license speculation, and even a three-way verdict with \texttt{undecidable} \citep{bayat_factbench_2024} refines the label space without consulting the contract: licensed invention is not merely undecidable, it is \emph{authorized}.

\subsection{Creativity evaluation leaves the oracle implicit}
Creativity is commonly defined as combining \emph{novelty} with \emph{value/appropriateness} under constraints \citep{runco_standard_2012}, but operationalizations vary sharply across tasks \citep{acar_creativity_2019, franceschelli_creativity_2024}.
Recent LLM work treats creativity as multi-dimensional, with benchmarks spanning divergent-thinking rubrics, creative-writing evaluation, and constrained problem solving; \textsc{CreativityPrism} typifies the trend by aligning heterogeneous tasks to shared quality, novelty, and diversity dimensions \citep{hou_creativityprism_2025, fein_litbench_2025}.
Such scores also remain evaluator-dependent: ratings shift with rubric design and rater background, changing model rankings even under identical prompts when using LLM-as-judge protocols \citep{ismayilzada_evaluating_2025, liu_g-eval_2023, zheng_judging_2023}.
From a \TR\ perspective, the deeper issue is that creativity benchmarks rarely treat the truth regime as an explicit variable: novelty can be rewarded without distinguishing \PD\ from \Hallu\ \citep{sui_confabulation_2024, padmakumar_measuring_2025}, even when factual grounding is part of the contract \citep{wang_user-centric_2024}.
Faithfulness metrics could detect such violations \citep{maynez_faithfulness_2020, chen_menli_2023}, but they are not integrated into creativity pipelines.

\subsection{The shared failure mode and the closest prior work}
When $\TR$ is left implicit, two symmetric errors arise.
Under a permissive regime ($\kappa{>}0$), strict factuality checks misclassify licensed speculation as \Hallu\ (false positives on \PD) \citep{cyriac_metrics_2025, sui_confabulation_2024}; under a strict regime ($\kappa{=}0$), novelty-oriented scores reward confident fabrications that inflate apparent originality (false negatives on \Hallu) \citep{liu_g-eval_2023, chen_menli_2023}.
Uncertainty and abstention methods address the strict-regime case: when support is insufficient, a system should hedge, refuse, or defer rather than assert.
Our claim is orthogonal: under $\kappa{>}0$, the question is not only whether the model should abstain, but whether the prompt licenses a non-assertoric or inventive move in the first place.
The closest existing steps narrow but do not close the gap.
Intent-hallucination evaluation decomposes a query into intent constraints (subject, action, qualifiers, quantity, and the like) and conditions the verdict on whether the response covers them, but it defines failure independently of factual accuracy, supplies no oracle, and treats even flagged invention as a misinterpretation rather than a licensed move: it encodes neither $O$ nor $\kappa$ \citep{hao_faithqa_2025}.
A recent benchmark splits hallucinations into ``intelligent'' and ``defective,'' the closest analogue to the \PD/\Hallu\ distinction, but it reads that label off the output's creativity and factual-deviation scores rather than off the prompt, so the label is determined by the text and cannot flip with the contract that should govern it \citep{yang_hicbench_2025}.
Closest in spirit, \citet{jiang_survey_2024} survey hallucination from a creativity perspective and ask whether hallucinated content can be creatively valuable; we differ by shifting the unit of analysis from the potential value of the content to the prompt-level truth regime that decides whether unsupported content is licensed at all.
None of these makes $(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ a scored variable; that layer is what we argue for.

\subsection{Why universal suppression of \Hallu\ is neither feasible nor desirable}
LLMs are probabilistic next-token predictors trained under incomplete supervision; they emit plausible continuations even when truth is underspecified, so eliminating \Hallu\ universally would require restricting the model or the task class.
Recent arguments support this floor \citep{banerjee_llms_2024, xu_hallucination_2024}, and strict regimes still reveal large residual error rates across short-form QA and truthfulness benchmarks \citep{mallen_when_2023, lin_truthfulqa_2022}.
Alignment tuning compounds the picture rather than resolving it: policies for refusal, hedging, and abstention learned during alignment tuning are themselves implicit, provider-chosen regime commitments that reshape when a model declines to diverge \citep{ouyang_training_2022}.
The same generative mechanism that produces unsupported claims also produces the novel hypotheses users request: \citet{sun_hallucinating_2024} formalize ``good hallucinations'' as reasoning paths that remain correct after convergence, and \citet{banerjee_does_2025} show that verification-style mitigation can preserve divergent thinking whereas some decoding interventions suppress it.
Because \Hallu\ has a structural floor and the same divergence drives \PD, the practical goal is not universal suppression but \TR-calibrated control: specify the regime, license divergence where the regime allows it, and enforce convergence before anything is presented as fact \citep{bang_hallulens_2025, he_shakespearean_2025}.

\section{Applying the Framework: Worked Verdicts}\label{sec:worked}

The audit shows that the contract is missing; this section shows that supplying it \emph{changes verdicts}.
We apply the decision rule of Section~\ref{sec:framework} by hand to four illustrative cases.
Cases~A--C each flip the verdict on the \emph{same} claim when one component of $(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ changes; Case~D is a scope boundary that, revealingly, does not flip.
Each instantiates a documented phenomenon: extrinsic-but-factual XSum hallucinations (A) \citep{maynez_faithfulness_2020}, fictional role-play (B and D) \citep{sadeq_mitigating_2024}, and FactBench's \texttt{undecidable} label (C) \citep{bayat_factbench_2024}.
The claim strings are schematic verdict tests rather than verbatim dataset items; their role is to isolate a verdict current pipelines leave underdetermined.
Table~\ref{tab:worked} summarizes the outcomes.

\paragraph{Case A: correct but unsupported (oracle shift).}
\citet{maynez_faithfulness_2020} document this pattern directly on XSum, where annotators label a summary span as an \emph{extrinsic but factual} hallucination: content not found in the source document yet factually correct.
An instance of the kind they document is a system summary that supplies the correct year of an event, accurate against world knowledge but absent from the article.
Decomposing the output to the claim level isolates this token from the model's other, incorrect additions.
Under the summarization regime $O=$ the source, $\sigma$ low, $\kappa{=}0$, the event year is truth-conditional and unsupported by $O$: it is \Hallu, even though it satisfies factuality (\Acc) against world knowledge.
Hold the output fixed and change only the oracle (``Tell me about this election,'' with $O=$ world knowledge), and the same token becomes supported, hence not \Hallu.
The flip is exactly the unresolved tension Maynez et al.\ flag, whether summaries should tolerate such factual hallucinations \citep{maynez_faithfulness_2020, kryscinski_evaluating_2020}; the framework attributes it to $O$ rather than to the text.

\paragraph{Case B: licensed invention that flips with the frame (license shift).}
Fictional role-play makes this routine \citep{sadeq_mitigating_2024}.
Take a single claim: that a certain café and its proprietor existed in 1920s Paris.
Under a historical-QA prompt ($O=$ world knowledge, $\kappa{=}0$), the café is unsupported and asserted as fact: it is \Hallu.
Under ``write a short story set in 1920s Paris'' ($\kappa{=}2$), the \emph{same} invented café is a pretended assertion the frame authorizes \citep{searle_logical_1975}: it is \PD\ if it serves the story.
Only the invention license changed; the claim, and its surface form, did not.
This is a flip a novelty-oriented score and a factuality score each miss from opposite sides \citep{hou_creativityprism_2025, sui_confabulation_2024}.

\paragraph{Case C: undecidable is not authorized (license shift).}
FactBench's VERIFY pipeline labels each verifiable unit \texttt{supported}, \texttt{unsupported}, or \texttt{undecidable}, the last when retrieved evidence is insufficient to confirm or refute it, and its hallucination score penalizes \texttt{undecidable} units at a fixed weight regardless of what the prompt asked for \citep{bayat_factbench_2024}.
A FactBench-style instance is a model claim that a tool ``supports multiple input formats,'' which a verifier may mark \texttt{undecidable} when support varies across versions.
That label tracks evidence availability, not the regime: under $\kappa{=}0$, asserting such a claim as established fact is \Hallu\ and the penalty is warranted; under $\kappa{=}1$ (flagged speculation), the same epistemically undecidable content, marked as a hypothesis, is \PD.
The epistemic status (undecidable) is constant; the verdict is set by the license $\kappa$, which a fixed undecidable penalty, and even a three-way verdict, cannot see.

\paragraph{Case D: a scope boundary that does not flip.}
Not every invention a fictional frame contains is licensed by it.
In the same 1920s-Paris story, suppose the model has a real, named historical figure assert a specific claim that figure never made, presented as biographical fact.
This is a genuine assertion about the real world, so it steps outside the declared frame: it fails the \emph{presented-on-the-regime's-terms} condition and stays \Hallu\ even under $\kappa{=}2$ \citep{searle_logical_1975}.
Case~D marks the edge of $\kappa$: a license reaches only the content inside its declared scope, which is what separates the invented café of Case~B from a fabricated real-world fact placed beside it.

\section{Research Agenda (Open Problems)}\label{sec:agenda}

If hallucination is regime-relative, evaluation must make the regime a first-class object.
The framework and the audit above turn that principle into concrete, tractable problems, organized from measurement foundations (items~1--4) to system-level design (items~5--7).
We present these as the paper's main forward contribution: not a finished evaluator, but the specification one would have to build.

\textbf{Operationalizing \TR\ as a scorable contract.}
A \TR-aware benchmark must annotate $O$, $\sigma$, and $\kappa$ independently rather than folding them into a task label.
The graded components are not hypothetical to measure: stylistic latitude already has established datasets and metrics \citep{pavlick_empirical_2016, liu_semisupervised_2022, osyvokon_pavlick_formality_2016}, and $\sigma$ could be placed on a graded scale via pairwise preference judgments fitted to comparative scoring models, though where its discrete levels should fall is a question for stylistic expertise that we leave open.
The open challenge is reliability: regime boundaries involve normative choices (what \emph{should} be grounded?) and creativity judgments are evaluator-dependent \citep{acar_creativity_2019, marco_reader_2025}; where agreement is lowest (typically on the usefulness dimension of \PD), the disagreement is itself informative and should be reported through soft labels rather than forced into consensus.

\textbf{Operationalizing \PD\ as a scored quantity.}
\PD\ is the framework's central new construct, yet Section~\ref{sec:framework} specifies it without saying how to measure it.
The three conditions of the labeling rule are not equally hard, and separating them turns \PD\ from a definition into a scoring protocol.
The \emph{licensing} condition is mechanical: it is read off the $\kappa$ annotation, so under $\kappa{=}0$ no claim can be \PD\ and the score is zero by construction, while under $\kappa{>}0$ one checks only whether the claim falls inside the licensed scope (for example, the declared fictional frame).
The \emph{presented-on-the-regime's-terms} condition is a stance check: whether a claim is marked as a hypothesis or asserted as verified fact is a verifiable, constraint-style property of the text, close to the format constraints that instruction-following evaluation already scores \citep{zhou_ifeval_2023}, and far more tractable than judging content quality.
The hard component is \emph{usefulness}, the standard novelty-with-value criterion of creativity assessment \citep{runco_standard_2012, acar_creativity_2019, diedrich_are_2015}: novelty admits automated proxies (semantic-distance and LLM-judge scoring of divergent output reach strong human correlation \citep{organisciak_beyond_2023}), and value can be aggregated from pairwise preference judgments onto a continuous scale.
A practical \PD\ score should therefore expose three sublabels: license-pass, stance-pass, and usefulness, with \PD\ assigned only when the first two pass and the third exceeds a regime-conditioned threshold.
Because usefulness is evaluator-dependent \citep{marco_reader_2025}, \PD\ should be reported as a graded, soft-labeled quantity rather than a hard count, with disagreement on the usefulness dimension recorded rather than averaged away.
This is a proposed instrument, not a validated one; establishing its reliability is itself part of the agenda.

\textbf{Inferring \TR\ from prompts, and separating it from instruction-following.}
Users rarely state a formal regime, so $\TR(p)$ must be inferred from prompt text \citep{wang_user-centric_2024, bodonhelyi_user_2024}, and calibration is critical: misreading a strict prompt as permissive suppresses warranted penalties, while the reverse penalizes legitimate \PD.
Inference must also be kept distinct from instruction-following fidelity: whether a model satisfied a verifiable format constraint is separately measurable \citep{zhou_ifeval_2023} and should not be confused with whether its content respects the regime, since an output can follow the requested form while violating $\kappa$.

\textbf{Metrics for \emph{excess} \Hallu\ under a given \TR.}
Under a regime-aware view what matters is not the raw hallucination count but the excess relative to the regime's tolerance, which requires severity weighting (a fabricated safety claim outweighs a date slip), claim- versus output-level aggregation, and regime-conditioned baselines (the acceptable rate differs between grounded summarization and brainstorming).
Severity-aware and risk-weighted proposals exist \citep{bang_hallulens_2025, wei_measuring_2024}, but none yet conditions severity on an explicit \TR\ contract.

\textbf{Robust \Hallu\ detection under stylistic variation.}
This is where $\sigma$ does its work in the framework: it changes no verdict, but it is the axis along which a verifier's \Hallu\ count can be corrupted.
Because raising $\sigma$ alone can move what a verifier flags, NLI-based verifiers risk conflating figurative language with unsupported claims \citep{chen_menli_2023, aynetdinov_semscore_2024}, a brittleness already visible in style-transfer metric meta-evaluations \citep{lai_multidimensional_2023, pauli_mind_2025}.
A diagnostic suite pairing identical propositional content with varying stylistic treatments \citep{jin_deep_2022} would separate verifier sensitivity to form from genuine divergence in content.

\textbf{Controlling \Hallu\ without collapsing \PD.}
Mitigation (constrained decoding, retrieval augmentation, post-hoc verification) typically targets \Hallu\ reduction without measuring collateral damage to creative quality, and the trade-off is asymmetric: verification-style interventions can preserve divergent thinking while some decoding constraints suppress it \citep{banerjee_does_2025, sun_hallucinating_2024, he_shakespearean_2025}.
A \TR-aware protocol should therefore report \emph{both} \Hallu\ reduction and \PD\ preservation under the same regime.

\textbf{Benchmarks crossing task $\times\,\sigma\,\times\,\kappa$.}
No existing benchmark systematically crosses task type, stylistic latitude, and invention license as independent variables; \textsc{CreativityPrism} and LitBench provide partial coverage but condition no scores on an explicit \TR\ \citep{hou_creativityprism_2025, fein_litbench_2025}.
A concrete next step is a claim-level annotation layer with labels such as supported, hallucination, productive divergence, unverifiable, and stylistic variation, evaluated as a research object in its own right, including whether it changes model rankings or instead chiefly sharpens error diagnosis.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Implications for evaluation practice}
The practical consequence of \TR-relative evaluation is a shift from defect counting to \emph{regime-conditioned diagnosis}.
Rather than asking ``how much does the output hallucinate?'', evaluation should ask ``does its divergence exceed what the \TR\ licenses?''
Three verdicts follow: \emph{aligned} (divergence matches the regime), \textit{excessive-}\textbf{\Hallu} (unsupported claims where grounding is required), and \textit{insufficient-}\textbf{\PD} (output too conservative for a permissive regime).
The first two read off the claim-level labels (a claim either is or is not \Hallu); the third does not, because too little divergence is an \emph{absence} that no per-claim label can record.
\textit{Insufficient-}\PD\ is therefore an output-level judgment that compares the output's licensed-divergence yield against what a permissive regime leads one to expect, the comparison creativity benchmarks already make when they penalize conservative outputs \citep{hou_creativityprism_2025}; the claim-level rule alone cannot deliver it.
The same measured rate therefore carries different meaning under strict and permissive contracts, so regime-specific baselines are not a refinement but a precondition for interpreting any score.
Even under permissive regimes, evaluation should still flag \emph{truth-commitment violations}, cases where invented content is presented as fact rather than as hypothesis or creative proposal \citep{bang_hallulens_2025, hou_creativityprism_2025}.

\subsection{Limitations of the position}
Our argument is conceptual, and five limitations bound it.
First, the framework is partly \emph{normative}: deciding what $O$ ought to be, and what a regime \emph{should} ground, is a value judgment about a task, not a measurement we can read off the text, so reasonable annotators will disagree on regime boundaries.
The framework treats that disagreement as signal rather than noise: where annotators diverge on a regime boundary, the divergence is recorded as a soft label instead of being forced to a single verdict (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}), which makes the normative choice auditable rather than hiding it inside an unstated default.
Second, $\TR(p)$ is an idealization that ambiguous prompts underdetermine; locating the contract at the prompt is a modeling choice, and inferring it reliably is an open problem (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}), not a solved preprocessing step.
Third, \PD\ is specified and we sketch a scoring protocol for it (Section~\ref{sec:agenda}), but we do not validate that protocol: its usefulness condition inherits the evaluator dependence of creativity assessment, so what we offer is a labeling rule and a measurement proposal, not an instrument shown to measure \PD\ reliably at scale.
Fourth, we argue this position rather than establishing it with a controlled study: the audit and worked verdicts show that the gap is real and that supplying $(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ resolves verdicts, but quantifying how much regime annotation changes evaluation outcomes at scale is left to future empirical work, which our preliminary observations only motivate.
Fifth, the framework is single-turn: it locates the contract in one prompt $p$ and does not model how a truth regime is established or revised across the turns of a dialogue, a restriction we leave for future work.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Hallucination is not a binary property of text but a violation relative to a prompt's truth regime.
The vocabulary introduced here, $\TR(p)=(O,\sigma,\kappa)$ together with \PD\ and \Hallu, makes explicit what current hallucination and creativity evaluations leave implicit: which oracle governs truth, how much stylistic latitude is granted, and whether new truth-conditional content is licensed.
Auditing widely used benchmarks shows that none of them encodes this contract, and applying the labeling rule by hand shows that supplying it flips verdicts the same output would otherwise receive by accident of protocol.
The agenda is therefore to move evaluation from defect counting to regime-conditioned diagnosis, so that it can penalize harmful fabrication under strict regimes while preserving productive divergence where exploration is the point.
The remaining work is to build the contract into benchmarks and to measure how much it changes what we conclude about models, but the first step is to stop scoring divergence as if every prompt asked for the same thing.

\appendixsection{Formal Claim-Level Decision Rule}\label{app:decision-rule}
\noindent\textbf{Input:} a prompt $p$, an output $y$, and an oracle $O$.\\
\noindent\textbf{Output:} a label for every atomic claim in $y$.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Specify the regime.} Infer or state $\TR(p)=(O,\sigma,\kappa)$: what is truth-conditional, what is evidence-bound, what is optionally speculative, and what (if anything) may be invented.
    \item \textbf{Decompose.} Split $y$ into atomic claims, ideally at claim-level granularity.
    \item \textbf{Record the evidence state} of each truth-conditional claim $c$ against $O$: \emph{supported} (entailed), \emph{unsupported} (contradicted or absent), or \emph{unverifiable} ($O$ holds no evidence either way).
    \item \textbf{Label each claim $c$:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item[supported] if $c$ is entailed by $O$; a non-truth-conditional claim is \emph{neither} (stylistic variation);
        \item[\Hallu] if $c$ is unsupported or unverifiable \emph{and} its departure is unlicensed ($\kappa{=}0$ or $c\notin\mathrm{scope}(\kappa)$), or is asserted as verified fact where $\kappa$ required a hedge or a declared frame;
        \item[\PD] if the departure is licensed ($\kappa>0$ and $c\in\mathrm{scope}(\kappa)$), presented on the regime's terms, and improves usefulness or appropriateness;
        \item[--] else \emph{neither}: licensed divergence that adds nothing useful.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}
\noindent Figure~\ref{fig:annotation-tree} renders this rule as a decision tree. The evidence state (supported / unsupported / unverifiable) is recorded but does not by itself fix the verdict: an unverifiable claim is \Hallu\ under $\kappa{=}0$ if asserted as fact, yet \PD\ under $\kappa{=}1$ if marked as a hypothesis (Case~C, Section~\ref{sec:worked}).
The supporting evidence for each step is given where the rule is introduced in Section~\ref{sec:framework}.
